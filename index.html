Goal Statement

The mission of this trigonometry portfolio is to assimilate evidence of progress, accomplishment, and useful information for the future to provide a good study tool and reference for many trigonometry related things such as identities and common methods of attacking various problems. The portfolio will also be a resource assembled to be used as a review tool for the final at the end of the year.

Most Useful Chart

The most useful chart for me was the ASTC Chart as I like to call it. The chart is drawn inside a circle, and the circle has 4 quadrants. Within the first quadrant, all trigonometric functions are positive, moving to the second quadrant on the left, only sine and its reciprocal cosecant are positive; in the third quadrant beneath, tangent and and cotangent; in the fourth quadrant to the right, only cosine and secant. The chart also helped me memorize some common special right triangles.


Converting from Radians Degrees

The best procedural process I learned was converting from radians to degrees and vice versa because we used it so many times on tests and quizzes. At first, I did not really understand the concept of radians and I could not understand and visualize what π/4 or 3π/7 was, so I decided to sit down and make a little step by step guide of how to convert between radians and degrees so I would learn some common radian fractions and so I could do well on the quizzes.


Right Triangle Trig at Work

One of the common triangles that we started with was the right triangle. The right triangle can be solved using the Law of Sines (left) or the Law of Cosines. The Law of Sines solves requires a minimum of 3 pieces of information about the triangle, either 2 sides and one angle, or 2 angles and one side. Using these three pieces, the remaining sides and angles can be calculated. The Law of Sines helped on the assessments we had because it allowed me to solve the triangles using proportions.

Law of Sines at Work

The problem shown to the left is a problem that uses the Law of Sines to solve any triangle with just 3 pieces of information. The Law of Sines applies to cases where 2 sides and one angle are given, or 2 angles and one side are given. In either case, the remaining information can be found using the Law of Sines.


Solving a Trig Identity

Verifying identities and solving them were some of the hardest things for me in this unit, and so I spent a lot of time practicing extra ones from the book and going over old homework problems, and an example of a solved identity is shown to the left. In this identity, cotangent and cosecant can be replaced with their full functions, and once that happens, the problem is a lot easier to solve. Just simplify the left side and both sides will end up with just cosine of x.


Real World Trig

In this problem, a person is looking up at a building, and obviously cannot drop a tape measure from the top to the bottom, but he knows how big the 4 pavers are between him and one side of the building. He also knows that the building is perfectly perpendicular and he is looking up at an angle of approximately 55°. Using trigonometry, the person can find the height of the building to be...only 81.40". Maybe he is looking at a model building.


Trig Function Transformations

In the image to the left, 4 transformations of the parent graph sine of x have been graphed. Between each set of dots is one cycle of the graph. In blue, is the graph 5sin(x), which increases the amplitude of the wave by 5 times the original 1. In the red graph, the function sin(x) + 5 moves the whole graph up 5. In the green graph with the function sin(4x), the graph's period is divided by 4, which makes each cycle appear smaller and more compact. Lastly, in the purple graph, the equation sin(x - 2) moves the whole graph to the right 2 units.
